Wangchuk's High-Stakes Fast Forces the Delhi High Court to Intervene Over Exam Integrity

Climate activist Sonam Wangchuk has entered the eighteenth day of an indefinite hunger strike at Jantar Mantar, pushing his body to the brink of medical failure. The protest targets systematic testing flaws and demands the resignation of Union Education Minister Dharmendra Pradhan over the National Eligibility cum Entrance Test leak scandal. With a public interest litigation now forcing the Delhi High Court to demand an immediate government response, this deep dive explains the volatile collision between non-violent protest, state force-feeding laws, and shifting political alliances. Readers will understand the legal mechanics of state intervention and the strategic weights driving the ongoing gridlock.

In Plain English

Imagine a town where the entry exam for the local hospital is systematically rigged, sparking widespread outrage among students who feel cheated. When the town leaders refuse to change the system, a respected community elder sits in the public square and refuses to eat until the system is fixed. As his health fails, the local judges must decide whether to respect his protest or send in guards to force food down his throat to save his life.

What drove Wangchuk to this fast?

Wangchuk launched his indefinite fast on June 28, 2026, alongside the Cockroach Janta Party, an organization founded by activist Abhijeet Dipke. The movement emerged from deep public anger surrounding systemic irregularities in the National Eligibility cum Entrance Test, widely known as NEET. The protesters argue that widespread paper leaks have compromised the futures of millions of students, demanding immediate accountability at the absolute top of the administrative ladder.

The primary objective of the fast is to force the resignation of Union Education Minister Dharmendra Pradhan. While typical student protests involve marches and campus demonstrations, Wangchuk's participation elevates the issue to a national moral crisis. By putting his own physical survival on the line, the activist is attempting to break through complete governmental apathy.

Why did his focus shift to education?

For years, Wangchuk was known primarily for leading mass protests demanding statehood and ecological protections for the fragile Himalayan region of Ladakh. However, in early July, representatives from the Leh Apex Body and the Kargil Democratic Alliance successfully resolved prolonged administrative gridlocks with the Ministry of Home Affairs regarding a proposed governance framework. With regional negotiations moving into a structured phase, Wangchuk publicly shifted his energy toward national systemic issues.

He explicitly noted that while progress was finally occurring on the Ladakh front, the government needed to turn its immediate attention to education reform. This pivot surprised some regional observers, but the more convincing reading is that Wangchuk views the integrity of national examinations as an existential issue for the country's youth. His transition from a regional climate champion to a national advocate for student rights highlights how deeply the testing scandal has fractured public trust across diverse demographics.

How dangerous is his current condition?

According to the official medical bulletin issued by the Cockroach Janta Party on July 15, 2026, Wangchuk is currently under round-the-clock medical vigilance. His total body weight has fallen to 57.15 kilograms, representing a massive drop of nearly nine kilograms since the fast began. Doctors recorded a weight loss of 400 grams within a single twenty-four hour window, indicating that his body has completely depleted its immediate fat reserves and has begun breaking down muscle tissue for survival.

Medical professionals note that while Wangchuk remains conscious and mentally alert, he is profoundly weak. His blood pressure was recently measured at 105/76, with his blood sugar dropping to 80 milligrams per deciliter. Although his oxygen saturation remains stable at ninety-seven percent, the prolonged lack of nutrients places him at severe risk for sudden cardiac arrhythmia or permanent organ damage if the fast continues.

Can courts order a protester force-fed?

The fast has sparked a profound legal battle within the Delhi High Court, where advocate Rakesh Kumar Saini filed an urgent public interest litigation. The petition requests that the bench, led by Chief Justice Devendra Kumar Upadhyaya and Justice Tejas Karia, order the government to forcibly transfer Wangchuk to a state hospital and administer liquid nutrients. Saini argues that while peaceful protest is a constitutional right, the state has a binding obligation to prevent a citizen from voluntarily ending their life.

The legal mechanism rests on a highly provocative interpretation of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023. The petition asserts that complete government inaction face-to-face with a visible hunger strike amounts to the abetment of suicide under Section 108. The legal logic pressures the state by arguing that allowing an activist to die in a public space makes the administration criminally complicit in his demise.

However, ordering medical force-feeding is deeply controversial. Human rights advocates argue that force-feeding a conscious, mentally alert individual violates personal autonomy and the fundamental right to bodily integrity. The court now faces the challenge of balancing its duty to preserve human life against the right of an individual to use their own body as an instrument of political dissent.

Why is the political opposition rallying?

The rapid deterioration of Wangchuk's health has turned Jantar Mantar into a critical rallying point for the country's political opposition. A broad coalition of senior political figures, including Congress Member of Parliament Shashi Tharoor, Trinamool Congress leader Mamata Banerjee, Samajwadi Party president Akhilesh Yadav, and Shiv Sena leader Uddhav Thackeray, have issued public appeals urging Wangchuk to preserve his life. Tharoor wrote an open letter stating that the fast had already successfully awakened the conscience of the nation.

This sudden convergence is not purely humanitarian. The opposition views Wangchuk's fast as a powerful mechanism to sustain political pressure on the ruling administration over its handling of the educational sector. By aligning themselves with a widely respected, non-partisan figure, opposition parties can channel student anger directly into Parliament without appearing entirely driven by partisan gain.

The momentum is set to intensify. Arvind Kejriwal, the national convenor of the Aam Aadmi Party, scheduled a high-profile visit to the protest site on July 16, 2026. Furthermore, organizers have announced a mass solidarity hunger strike, culminating in a planned march to Parliament on July 20 to directly confront lawmakers.

What if the government remains silent?

The administration currently faces a damaging strategic gridlock. Accepting Wangchuk's demands by removing the Education Minister would be viewed as an admission of systemic failure, potentially exposing the government to further accountability campaigns. On the other hand, maintaining absolute silence while a prominent national figure physically wastes away in the capital risks sparking widespread public unrest.

The more convincing reading is that the government is waiting for judicial intervention to provide a clean exit strategy. If the Delhi High Court orders medical hospitalization, the state can intervene under the guise of legal compliance rather than political capitulation. This would remove Wangchuk from the public stage at Jantar Mantar while effectively defusing the immediate visual power of the hunger strike.

Yet, shifting the battle from the streets to a hospital ward will not resolve the underlying crisis. If the government fails to initiate a transparent dialogue regarding the core testing irregularities, the planned march to Parliament on July 20 will likely transform into a much broader confrontation, turning student grievances into a sustained national movement.

Conclusion

The standoff at Jantar Mantar has evolved from a local protest into a complex national crisis, placing the administration under immense structural pressure. Whether resolved through judicial intervention or formal legislative concession, the underlying demand for exam integrity remains an urgent priority for student rights advocates across India.
